By Joanna Buczkowska-McCumber

By 2030, the ocean economy is expected to reach $3-trillion an-nually, yet less than one per cent of global venture funding flows into ocean-related ventures. At first glance, it’s tempting to frame this as an “emerging” economy. But that misses a deeper truth: oceans have always been economies. For millennia, Indigenous peoples and coastal communities worldwide have sustained their livelihoods through fishing, seaweed harvest-ing, and marine stewardship; these practices remain among the oldest and most resilient forms of eco-nomic life.

What feels new today is how these long-standing ocean economies are being reframed. One clear shift is the rise of ocean-based climate solutions. The ocean is no longer viewed solely as a victim of warm-ing seas, but as an active tool in the fight against climate change. At the same time, ocean resources are breaking into new industries. Seaweed is moving beyond food into applications ranging from regenerative agriculture and sus-tainable packaging to bioplastics, construction materials, fertilizers, and methane-reducing additives for livestock. Canadian ventures such as BioLabMate Composite Inc, Alaagi Inc. and SEVO Bioscience and other OSP alumni are experiment-ing with processing and distribu-tion systems that could transform seaweed from a niche product into an industrial material.

Another development is the push for real accountability in ocean ac-tivity. For centuries, oceans have been described as ungovernable because they were invisible. That is changing fast. Platforms like eOceans now map vessel traffic in real time, while companies such as OnDeck AI make it possible to detect and analyze objects, behav-iors, and events in marine footage instantly—no training data re-quired. The ocean is no longer un-governable; data is bringing it into focus and setting a new baseline for accountability.

These shifts are also reshaping how capital moves into the sector. Financing models are adapting to the realities of operating on the wa-ter, longer timelines, heavier infra-structure, and regulatory complex-ity. In Canada, government-backed programs and regional funds are experimenting with blended cap-ital to de-risk early investments, while globally, specialized investors such as Propeller VC to Katapult Ocean are testing models that back ventures traditional capital often ignores. Startups are also leaning on commercialization pilots with industry partners to prove viability before scaling, creating financial pathways that treat stewardship not as a liability but as a source of long-term value.

And perhaps most importantly, the ecosystem is beginning to cohere. Ocean entrepreneurship is evolv-ing from scattered experiments into something more connected. Researchers, Indigenous leaders, investors, and founders are be-ginning to work across silos with shared priorities.

The real weight of ocean startups isn’t in the novelty of their tech-nologies but in the pressure they place on established markets. Ship-ping, fisheries, and offshore energy

already dominate the ocean econ-omy, and their business models were built for throughput, not resil-ience. Startups entering this space don’t just bring tools; they bring expectations. Real-time data plat-forms expose practices once hid-den. Seaweed ventures push agri-culture and packaging industries to rethink inputs. New aquaculture technologies show that efficiency and sustainability can align. Each one chips away at the assumption that growth and stewardship are incompatible.

That’s why these early ventures matter more than their size sug-gests. They are forcing incum-bents, investors, and policymakers to decide whether the next chap-ter of the ocean economy repeats the extractive logic of the past or builds new rules around regener-ation. There is little doubt that the ocean economy will grow, but what remains open is who shapes it, and whether the future being written on the water expands resilience or simply extends risk.
