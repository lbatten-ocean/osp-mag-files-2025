When Vancouver-based archi-tectural designer and biobased material designer Annie Dahan walks along the shoreline, she sees more than just kelp and seaweed swaying in the surf. She sees the build-ing blocks of a more sustainable future.

Dahan is the founder of Seacork Studio, a biobased material startup transforming locally har-vested invasive seaweed into fully compostable, carbon-stor-ing acoustic panels. Lightweight, sound-absorbing, and easy to in-stall or replace, Seacork’s panels are designed to outperform con-ventional petroleum-based ma-terials while working in harmony with the ocean ecosystem.

“Seaweeds are regenerative and inherently beautiful. Growing along coastlines worldwide, they offer a glob-ally scalable solution — one that demands thoughtful and sustainable manage-ment,” Dahan said. “With these qualities, seaweed became the perfect foun-dation for creating a new, regenerative construction materi-al.”

Her path to sea-weed-based soundproof-ing began with a Bachelor’s degree in Environmental Scienc-es from McGill University and a Master’s in Architecture from the University of British Colum-bia. She spent years advising architects and designing

projects through a sustainabil-ity lens, developing a passion for regenerative architecture and a curiosity about bio-based materials. Living near the Pacific coast, she began experimenting with seaweeds creating a light-weight, porous material, which naturally lends itself to sound absorption. That curiosity even-tually led to the invention of

Seacork’s patent-pending ma-terial, which not only dampens noise but actively stores more carbon dioxide than it emits over its lifecycle.

“I started experimenting in my kitchen with local seaweeds, testing how far I could push its potential, while learning about its biochemistry and its import-ant roles in the Salish Sea eco-system. It eventually became clear this was more than a de-sign exercise — it could be a real solution.”

Dahan’s entrepreneurial journey has been deeply connected to the Ocean Startup Project (OSP). She first joined the OSP com-munity through the Ocean Idea Challenge, where she refined her concept, and later the Ocean Startup Challenge, which helped her es-tablish a solid business foundation. As an alum-na of both programs, she went on to secure Boost Funding from OSP to accelerate her scale-up efforts.

Earlier this year, she won COAST’s first an-nual pitch competi-tion, supported by OSP, earning $10,000 in cash to grow production and supply upcoming pilot projects.

Paula Mendonça, Executive Direc-tor of the Ocean Startup Project, says Dahan’s work is exactly the kind of impact-driven innovation OSP exists to support:

“Annie’s journey with Seacork is a perfect example of why the Ocean Startup Project exists—to help visionary entrepreneurs turn bold, ocean-based ideas into viable businesses,” Men-donça said.

“She has been part of our com-munity since the early days, coming through both the Ocean Idea Challenge and Ocean Start-up Challenge, and most recently earning Boost Funding to help her scale. Seacork’s innovative use of seaweed to create sus-tainable, carbon-negative acous-tic panels has the potential to transform not just one industry,

but the way we think about ma-terials altogether. We are thrilled to celebrate Annie’s success as part of our five-year milestone, and can’t wait to see where she takes this next.”

For Dahan, the work is about more than creating a better acoustic panel — it’s about

redefining the relationship be-tween industry and nature.

“Every material has a story, and I believe that story should start and end with respect for the ecosystem it comes from. With Seacork, the ocean isn’t just a re-source—it’s a partner,” she said.

Her hope is that Seacork will not only lead the way in sustainable sounddampening, but also in-spire other innovators to look to the ocean for materials that re-generate rather than deplete.

As the Ocean Startup Project celebrates its fifth anniversary, startups like Seacork embody the potential of ocean entre-preneurship—driven by vision, grounded in science, and built in collaboration with the very ecosystems they aim to protect.
