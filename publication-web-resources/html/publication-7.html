We hear about ocean health all the time—rising sea levels, microplastics, pollution, and de-bris—but what does cleaning the ocean actually involve? Ocean AID, supported by the Ocean Startup Project, is leading the way in turn-ing big ideas into real action by tackling one of the ocean’s most stubborn and costly problems: removing ghost gear.

Fishing nets, ropes, traps, and other equipment that have been lost or abandoned in the ocean wreak havoc long after their in-tended use. Alarmingly, these remnants of human activity ac-count for about 70 per cent of the ocean’s plastic pollution. Drift-ing for years, they trap marine life, damage fragile ecosystems, and eventually break down into harmful microplastics. Recov-ery efforts have been ongoing for decades, but they are costly, time-consuming, and limited in scale. The sheer size of the ocean

makes locating and retrieving this debris a monumental task.

That’s where Andrew Polanyi and Archit Kumar, co-founders of Ocean AID, step in. Their company is bringing forward an innovative solution with advanced technol-ogy designed to detect and clas-sify underwater objects in real time, dramatically improving the speed and accuracy of cleanup missions.

Polanyi’s own path to this work is unconventional. Raised in To-ronto, far from the ocean, he first discovered entrepreneurship at age 16, selling juice at farmers’ markets. His summers on Vancou-ver Island, spent fishing with his grandparents, deepened his con-nection to the ocean. Later, with a degree in software engineering from the University of Victoria, he

merged that passion with technical expertise, ultimately co-founding Ocean AID.

Based in Victoria, British Colum-bia, Ocean AID is a B2B ocean tech company specializing in automatic target recognition systems for marine use. “We specialize in creating automat-ic target recognition systems,” Polanyi explains, “to help vessels and marine missions detect and classify underwater objects with high efficiency and accuracy.” Their real-time signal process-ing means data is analyzed as it is collected, allowing crews to make almost immediate deci-sions—an enormous leap from traditional systems that require post-mission analysis before re-trieval can even begin. With appli-cations ranging from detecting ghost gear and derelict vessels to monitoring fish and marine life, their tech-nology is both versa-tile and sustainable.

Polanyi’s vision for Ocean AID be-gan with his participation in the Ocean Startup Project’s Ocean Community Climate Challenge in 2022. Early funding and mentor-ship allowed him and Kumar to test their ideas using his grand-father’s boat. Like many startups, the journey hasn’t been without challenges. When the Canadian Government’s Ghost Gear Fund was not renewed in 2024, Ocean AID faced a sudden gap in the market and had to pivot quickly. With support from mentors, Po-lanyi began cold-calling First Na-tions and other organisations to explore new opportunities. That persistence led to a contract with a First Nation in Prince Ru-pert, where Ocean AID’s technolo-gy was used to detect shoreline debris as part of a beach cleanup initiative.

Now, the company is building mo-mentum. Their Marlin ATR system targets underwater objects like

ghost gear, while the Eagle ATR system uses aerial video to scan shorelines and surface waters. They are also developing models to monitor fish, seabirds, and oth-er marine life, opening doors to new ecological insights. Looking ahead, Ocean AID plans to expand into the fishing sector, offering tools that could improve efficien-

cy and reduce bycatch for the more than 170,000 vessels oper-ating in British Columbia alone.

Polanyi is clear about the scale of his ambitions. He envisions Ocean AID becoming a global leader in marine monitoring technology within five years. It’s a bold goal, but one that feels attainable giv-en their trajectory and the urgen-cy of the ocean’s challenges.

Ghost gear is one of the largest contributors to plastic pollution in the ocean, but with compa-nies like Ocean AID driving innova-tion, there is hope. By combining cutting-edge technology with a mission rooted in sustainability, Andrew Polanyi and his team are proving that entrepreneurship can play a direct role in cleaning and protecting our oceans.
