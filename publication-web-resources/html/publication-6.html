If proof is needed that ocean sci-ence can rewrite everyday ma-terials, look to PhyCo. The Vancou-ver-based, women-led venture is building a petroleum-free, sea-weed-based plastic designed to do the hard, unglamorous work in our food system — then safely return them to the earth. Their focus is the farm: mulch

films, wraps, and liners that must perform under stress yet too often linger as waste. PhyCo’s answer is a compostable film that does its job in the field and then responsibly disappears.

Co-founders Ranah Chavoshi (CEO), a marine biologist with expertise in aquatic toxicology and phycol-ogy, and Dr. Stacey Goldberg (CSO), a biomedical scientist, bring a rare blend of environmental insight and materials rigour. Chavoshi’s vantage point on microplastics and ecosys-tem health meets Goldberg’s de-composition-centric approach to polymer design. Together, they are tackling the two hardest questions in bioplastics: can it match legacy performance, and will it truly break down where people actually com-post?

“Seaweed lets us design plastics that work with nature, not against it. If our film doesn’t perform for farm-ers and compost at the end of life, it doesn’t ship,” Chavoshi said.

From inception, the team set non-negotiables: no petrole-um-based plasticizers, non-toxic ingredients, and end-of-life path-ways that don’t rely on idealized in-dustrial facilities. Their R&D centers on thin films for agricultural and food-system use, engineered with manufacturability in mind—extru-sion and blown-film compatibility rather than lab-scale novelty. The goal is “earth-digestible” materials: films that hold up through plant-ing, harvesting, or packing, then break down predictably in home or municipal compost streams.

The founders have paired materials inno-vation with a thought-ful supply-chain strat-egy. PhyCo operates with a “two-coast” foot-print—research depth and seaweed science in British Columbia, with growing ties to producers and partners in Atlantic Canada. PhyCo’s steady momentum also re-flects the value of Canada’s ocean-innovation pipeline. The company’s relationship with the Ocean Startup Project (OSP) has unfolded in a clear, founder-cen-tric progression. Early proof came in September 2022, when PhyCo had entered Lab2Market Oceans, pressure-testing assumptions with structured customer discov-ery and market interviews. Then, in June 2023, they won the Ocean Idea Challenge, which validated the problem-solution fit and sharp-ened the early customer thesis.

In November 2023, the team was named among the national Ocean Startup Challenge winners, unlock-ing mentorship, non-dilutive cap-ital, and a network of blue econ-omy operators. Most recently, in March 2025, PhyCo received Boost funding to accelerate key mile-stones — species diversification, pilot deployments, and readiness for manufacturing scale-up. The throughline is consistent support at each rung of the commercializa-tion ladder.

“Our design brief has two impera-tives: performance in farmers’ hands and safety at the end of life. The planet can’t compromise on one, and growers can’t compro-mise on the other,” Goldberg said.

What distinguishes PhyCo in a crowded biomaterials field is its buyer-back approach. Rath-er than chasing consumer gloss, the company targets the largest, least visible plastic load on farms — applications where reliability, cost, and disposal logistics decide adoption. If a film saves labour at removal, avoids landfill fees, and eases compliance for growers and

waste managers, the sustainable choice becomes the obvious busi-ness decision. That is the wedge to displace conventional polymers at scale.

Momentum beyond the lab is build-ing. The team is preparing tight pi-lot loops with growers, tuning film properties for specific jobs—from soil contact and humidity manage-ment to durability across seasonal temperature swings—and lining up third-party compostability ver-ification under real-world condi-tions. Parallel work continues on seaweed supply, with attention to species selection, cultivation best practices, and domestic biorefin-ing capacity.

“PhyCo is founder-driven science at its best — community-minded, commercially rigorous, and re-lentless about impact,” said Paula Mendonça, Executive Director at the Ocean Startup Project. “This startup has grown with care and conviction. Each step — from early validation to today’s pilot work — has been thoughtful, measurable, and grounded in real farmer needs. It’s been a privilege to support that kind of steady, principled progress.”

As the Ocean Startup Project marks five years, PhyCo reads like a case study in what an ecosystem can unlock: identify exceptional found-ers, validate early with customers, and stay close through the messy middle of scale-up. When Chavoshi and Goldberg succeed, they won’t just replace a sliver of farm plas-tics; they will reset expectations for what plastics can be in Canada — functional, circular, and locally grown. The future they are building is one where ocean-grown materi-als work hard on the farm and then, quietly and safely, disappear.
